Who We Are

A National Humanitarian & Development Agency Rooted in Community

Headquartered in Juba, Republic of South Sudan, GOD SPEAKING TO THE MANKIND'S CHURCH AND DEVELOPMENT AGENCY (GSM-CDA) was officially formed and registered on 30 January 2024 as a national non-governmental organization (NGO).

GSM-CDA was founded in response to the increasing humanitarian, social, economic, environmental, and development challenges affecting communities throughout South Sudan—including armed conflict, displacement, flooding, drought, disease outbreaks, food insecurity, economic instability, and the adverse impacts of climate change.

The Agency serves all people regardless of ethnicity, tribe, religion, gender, age, political affiliation, disability, or social status, ensuring that humanitarian assistance is provided solely on the basis of need.

Organizational Background & History

GOD SPEAKING TO THE MANKIND'S CHURCH AND DEVELOPMENT AGENCY (GSM-CDA) is a national, non-profit, non-governmental humanitarian and development organization established to improve the quality of life of vulnerable communities across South Sudan. The Agency was officially formed and registered in Juba, Republic of South Sudan, on 30 January 2024.

GSM-CDA was founded in response to the increasing humanitarian, social, economic, environmental, and development challenges affecting communities throughout South Sudan. Recognizing the need for a nationally led organization that combines humanitarian relief with sustainable development, GSM-CDA was established to provide timely emergency assistance while promoting long-term resilience, peace, and inclusive development.

The Agency believes that every person deserves the opportunity to live in dignity, free from hunger, poverty, disease, violence, discrimination, environmental degradation, and preventable suffering. Guided by principles of humanity, impartiality, neutrality, independence, accountability, integrity, and respect for human rights, GSM-CDA works alongside communities, government institutions, faith-based organizations, civil society organizations, development partners, and the private sector to create lasting positive change.

Current Projects in Bentiu & Across South Sudan

IDP Support & Protection

Emergency Support for Displaced Families – UN/IDP Camp, Bentiu

GSM-CDA is on the ground inside the UN-managed IDP camp in Bentiu, fighting to protect the rights and dignity of displaced people. We provide emergency food distributions, clean water, hygiene kits, health advocacy, and environmental protection services to families uprooted by conflict and environmental disasters. We also advocate for better living conditions and hold authorities accountable for the well‑being of the camp’s most vulnerable residents.

3,250 families reached of 5,000 target (65%)

📍 Bentiu IDP Camp, Unity State

Education & WASH

Safe Water Awareness & Filtration Program – Bentiu

Working to stop people from drinking contaminated and dirty water in Bentiu by distributing water filtration kits, conducting community hygiene education, and training local health champions. This initiative addresses waterborne diseases that disproportionately affect children, pregnant women, and the elderly in flood-affected and oil-polluted areas of Unity State.

Active – Enrolling Community Health Champions Now

📍 Bentiu Town & Surrounding Payams, Unity State

Water & Sanitation

Livestock & Water Source Protection – Bentiu Oil Fields

Working to stop animals from dying due to poisoning from poor petroleum mining practices in the Bentiu region. This project installs protective barriers around contaminated water sources, provides alternative watering points for livestock, conducts soil and water testing, and advocates for responsible environmental practices in oil-producing areas of Unity State to protect both animal and human health.

75% Complete – 8 of 10 Critical Sites Secured

📍 Oil-Affected Areas, Bentiu, Unity State

"Born into a Toxic Legacy"

⚠️ Featured Investigation

A Child Born Badly: The Hidden Health Crisis of Poor Petroleum Mining in Bentiu

Kuach Maale Payam, Guit County — In the small village of Kuach Maale, deep in Guit County, a baby boy was born in early 2026 with severe congenital abnormalities that doctors say are consistent with exposure to toxic environmental contaminants. His mother, Nyenhial Gatluak Dhuor, and father, Both Riek Bol, had lived their entire lives within sight of the nearby oil field, surrounded by unprotected waste pits, contaminated water sources, and air thick with the fumes of gas flaring.

"I drank the water that was available. I breathed the air that was there. I did not know it could harm my unborn child," Nyenhial told GSM-CDA field staff during a home visit, her voice heavy with grief. Her son died just a few weeks after birth — one of a growing number of children in Guit and across Unity State born with birth defects, respiratory illnesses, skin conditions, and developmental delays that community members and health workers attribute to decades of poorly regulated petroleum extraction.

GSM-CDA's investigation has documented at least 47 cases of children under five in the Bentiu and Guit area presenting with unusual health conditions—including congenital limb deformities, chronic respiratory distress, severe skin rashes, and neurological symptoms—over the past two years. Many families, including Nyenhial and Both's neighbors, report that their livestock have also suffered mysterious deaths and stillbirths after grazing near oil-contaminated land and drinking from polluted water points.

"The evidence is overwhelming," said a GSM-CDA environmental health officer working in the region. "We are seeing a pattern of disease and suffering that is directly linked to the environmental degradation caused by poor petroleum mining practices. The tragic death of Nyenhial's son is not an isolated incident — this is a public health emergency, a child protection crisis, and a human rights issue all at once."

GSM-CDA is calling for urgent action: independent environmental and health assessments in oil-affected communities, immediate provision of clean water and medical care for affected families, stricter enforcement of environmental regulations on petroleum operators, and comprehensive support for children born with pollution-related health conditions. The Agency is also expanding its on-the-ground programmes in Guit and Bentiu to address the most pressing needs while advocating for systemic change.
